This page introduces the main regulatory and compliance frameworks affecting shipping and bunkering. It is written for people new to the maritime industry.

## 1. Why regulations matter

Regulations exist to protect people, ships, cargo, and the environment. Global standards create a common language so ships can trade safely between many countries and ports. Compliance is not just legal paperwork — it directly affects how vessels are built, how fuel is handled, and how operations are run.

Think of standards as a shared blueprint for safe design and operation. Most day-to-day procedures in bunkering trace back to a specific standard, code, or regulation.

## 2. International Maritime Organization (IMO)

The International Maritime Organization (IMO) is the United Nations agency responsible for the safety and environmental performance of international shipping. It creates the global rules that ships must follow, operating through committees and member states.

**Key IMO committees and bodies:**

- **MSC** (Maritime Safety Committee) – Addresses safety: SOLAS, collision regulations, search and rescue.
- **MEPC** (Marine Environment Protection Committee) – Addresses environmental issues: air pollution, GHG, oil pollution.
- **FAL** (Facilitation Committee) – Addresses documentation and port formalities.

Official website: imo.org

## 3. Core IMO conventions

### 3.1 SOLAS (Safety of Life at Sea)

The primary international treaty on maritime safety. Covers ship structure, stability, fire protection, life-saving appliances, navigation, and radio communications. Almost all commercial ships must comply.

### 3.2 MARPOL (Marine Pollution)

The International Convention for the Prevention of Pollution from Ships. Divided into six Annexes, each covering a different type of pollution:

| Annex | Subject |
|---|---|
| Annex I | Oil pollution |
| Annex II | Noxious liquid substances in bulk |
| Annex III | Harmful substances in packaged form |
| Annex IV | Sewage |
| Annex V | Garbage |
| Annex VI | Air pollution — sulphur, NOₓ, ODS, GHG |

**Annex VI is most relevant to bunkering because it:**

- Sets the global sulphur cap (0.5% since 1 January 2020 — "IMO 2020")
- Establishes Emission Control Areas (ECAs) where stricter limits apply (0.1% sulphur, NOₓ Tier III)
- Introduces energy efficiency measures: EEDI (for newbuilds), EEXI, and CII (operational)
- Requires the Ship Fuel Oil Consumption Data Collection System — Regulation 22A reporting

### 3.3 STCW (Standards of Training, Certification, and Watchkeeping)

Sets minimum qualification standards for seafarers: certification, rest hours, watchkeeping, and competency requirements. Relevant to ensuring crew are qualified to handle specific fuels (e.g., LNG or methanol require additional certification).

### 3.4 IMO IGF Code

The International Code of Safety for Ships Using Gases or Other Low-Flashpoint Fuels. Sets safety requirements for ships fuelled by LNG, methanol, or other low-flashpoint fuels. Covers tank design, vent systems, hazardous zones, bunkering procedures, and crew training.

## 4. Emission Control Areas (ECAs)

ECAs are designated sea areas where stricter limits on air emissions apply.

**Current ECAs include:**

- North Sea and Baltic Sea (EU/IMO)
- North American ECA (US/Canada coasts)
- US Caribbean Sea ECA
- Chinese coastal ECA (Chinese domestic shipping)

Ships in ECAs must use fuel with ≤0.1% sulphur, or have an approved equivalent (e.g., exhaust gas cleaning systems / scrubbers). NOₓ Tier III limits also apply to new ships in IMO-designated ECAs.

## 5. Singapore standards (SS648, SS600, SS709)

Singapore is one of the world's largest bunkering hubs, and the Maritime and Port Authority of Singapore (MPA) mandates specific standards for bunker operations. These are among the most detailed and operationally specific bunkering standards in the world.

### 5.1 SS648 — Code of Practice for Bunker Mass Flow Metering

SS648 governs the use of Mass Flow Meters (MFMs) as the custody transfer measurement device for conventional marine fuel deliveries in Singapore.

**Key requirements:**

- All bunker barges in Singapore must use MPA-approved MFMs for measurement.
- MFMs must be type-approved, calibrated, and sealed.
- Measurement uncertainty must meet defined limits.
- The MFM reading is the authoritative quantity for the eBDN — soundings may not be used to override the MFM.
- Real-time data from MFMs must feed into the digital bunkering system and MPA's reporting platform.

SS648 has two parts:
- SS 648-1 – Requirements for the MFM system
- SS 648-2 – Requirements for the data interface and reporting

### 5.2 SS600 — Code of Practice for Bunkering

SS600 covers the broader bunkering operation in Singapore: safe procedures for connecting, pre-delivery checks, pumping, measurement, documentation, and completion. It complements SS648 by defining the operational procedures that accompany MFM-based measurement.

### 5.3 SS709 — Fuel Grade Codes

SS709 defines the fuel type codes used in Singapore's digital bunkering ecosystem. These codes standardise how fuel grades are described across systems (suppliers, platforms, and MPA's reporting system). Fuel codes cover conventional fuels (VLSFO, HSFO, MGO, MDO), biofuel blends (BIO-VLSFO, BIO-MGO, HVO variants), methanol grades (MMA, MMB, MMC), and others.

### 5.4 MPA digital bunkering and eBDN

MPA requires electronic Bunker Delivery Notes (eBDNs) for all conventional fuel deliveries in Singapore. The eBDN system:

- Generates the BDN from MFM data automatically
- Requires digital signatures from both the receiving vessel and the supplier
- Transmits data to MPA's regulatory platform (B2G — business-to-government)
- Provides traceability from measurement through to reporting

## 6. Sanctions and trade restrictions

Sanctions are political or economic measures imposed by countries or international bodies. In maritime, they restrict who you can trade with, which cargoes are permitted, and which ships or ports are off-limits.

**Key sanctioning bodies:**

- **OFAC** (US Office of Foreign Assets Control) – ofac.treasury.gov
- **European Union** – EU Sanctions Map
- **UN Security Council** – Global sanctions applicable to all UN member states
- **UK OFSI** – UK sanctions regime, now separate from the EU post-Brexit

**How sanctions affect bunkering:**

- You must identify the ultimate beneficial owner of a vessel, not just the registered owner.
- You must check the flag, port of call, and cargo for sanctions exposure.
- Many companies run automated sanctions screening on vessels, companies, and ports before approving any transaction.
- Sanctions exposure changes constantly — vessels can be added to lists after a nomination is placed.

> **Key takeaway:** Sanctions checks are as critical as technical checks. "One and done" is not sufficient — they must be repeated at multiple points in the workflow.

## 7. Carbon emissions and climate regulations

Shipping is under increasing pressure to reduce greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions. Several regulatory frameworks now carry real financial and operational consequences — not just reporting obligations.

### 7.1 IMO GHG strategy and net-zero goal

IMO adopted a Revised GHG Strategy (2023) targeting:

- Net-zero GHG emissions from international shipping by or around 2050
- Indicative checkpoints: 20% reduction by 2030, 70% reduction by 2040 (vs 2008 baseline)

**Implementation via MARPOL Annex VI measures:**

- **EEXI** (Energy Efficiency Existing Ship Index) – One-time compliance measurement for existing ships
- **CII** (Carbon Intensity Indicator) – Annual operational rating (A–E) used to benchmark and improve efficiency
- **Mid- and long-term measures** – Including a potential GHG levy and fuel standard (under negotiation)

### 7.2 EU ETS (Emissions Trading System) — Maritime

The EU ETS was extended to cover shipping from 1 January 2024. Ship operators must surrender carbon allowances (EUAs) for a percentage of CO₂ emissions on voyages involving EU ports:

- 40% of emissions covered in 2024
- 70% in 2025
- 100% from 2026 onwards
- Also covers CH₄ and N₂O from 2026

This creates a direct financial cost linked to fuel choice and voyage efficiency.

### 7.3 FuelEU Maritime

FuelEU Maritime (Regulation EU 2023/1805) sets maximum GHG intensity limits for energy used on board ships calling at EU ports. Limits tighten progressively from 2025 to 2050. Ships that exceed the limit face a penalty. Ships that outperform can "bank" or trade surplus performance.

This regulation drives demand for low-GHG fuels (LNG, methanol, biofuels, and eventually ammonia and hydrogen).

### 7.4 Regional schemes

- **California Air Resources Board (CARB)** – Controls emissions from ships within California's regulated area; requires use of low-sulphur fuel and shore power at berth.
- **China ETS** – China is expanding its ETS; maritime inclusion under discussion.

## 8. Port state control (PSC) and enforcement

Port state control is the mechanism by which flag state compliance is verified in practice. PSC inspectors board foreign ships in national ports and check for compliance with SOLAS, MARPOL, STCW, and other conventions.

**Common deficiencies found during PSC inspections include:**

- Fuel oil record book irregularities
- Failure to carry compliant fuel
- MARPOL sampling and documentation issues
- Crew certification issues

Serious deficiencies can result in vessel detention until resolved.

## 9. How to use this knowledge

You do not need to be a lawyer, but you should know which frameworks exist and where to look for more detail.

When you encounter a new regulation or standard:

1. Is it global (IMO) or regional (EU, US, Singapore, China)?
2. Does it affect fuel type, fuel quality, documentation, reporting, or voyage planning?
3. Who in the organisation owns this topic — compliance, legal, operations, or product?

Use the official links in this guide as your starting point. Over time, focus on understanding how rules show up in day-to-day workflows: contracts, eBDNs, system requirements, and customer questions.

## 10. Quick recap

- **IMO** via MARPOL, SOLAS, STCW, and the IGF Code sets the global baseline for ship safety and environmental rules.
- **MARPOL Annex VI** governs sulphur caps, ECAs, and GHG efficiency measures (EEXI, CII).
- **Singapore standards** (SS648, SS600, SS709) provide detailed, practical rules for bunkering in one of the world's key fuel hubs.
- **Sanctions regimes** (OFAC, EU, UN, UK) control who you can legally do business with — checks must be repeated throughout the workflow.
- **Climate regulations** (IMO GHG strategy, EU ETS, FuelEU) are reshaping fuel choices and voyage economics with real financial consequences.
