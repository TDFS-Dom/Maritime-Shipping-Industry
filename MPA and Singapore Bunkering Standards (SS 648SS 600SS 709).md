Singapore is the world's largest bunkering hub by volume, handling over 50 million metric tonnes of marine fuel per year. The Maritime and Port Authority of Singapore (MPA) has developed a detailed regulatory and standards framework that governs how bunkering is conducted in Singapore waters. This page explains that framework and the key standards: SS 648, SS 600, SS 524, and SS 709.

## 1. Why Singapore matters in bunkering

Singapore's significance stems from:

- Geographic position at the crossroads of East-West and North-South shipping routes
- Depth and efficiency of its port infrastructure (Jurong, Pulau Bukom, the anchorages)
- Regulatory leadership: Singapore has pioneered mass flow metering (MFM), digital bunkering (eBDN), and MPA's Business-to-Government (B2G) digital reporting system
- Market depth: large number of licensed bunker suppliers, surveyors, and service providers

Because many global shipping companies bunker regularly in Singapore, Singapore's standards effectively set a benchmark that influences practices elsewhere.

## 2. MPA's role in bunkering regulation

The Maritime and Port Authority of Singapore (MPA) regulates bunkering in Singapore under the Merchant Shipping Act. Key functions include:

- **Licensing bunker suppliers:** only MPA-licensed suppliers may deliver bunkers in Singapore port limits
- **Approving bunker barges and MFM systems:** barges must meet MPA requirements; their MFM systems must be type-approved and verified
- **Setting operational standards:** SS 648 and related standards are adopted as legally binding conditions of the bunker supplier licence
- **Digital reporting (B2G):** MPA operates a digital reporting platform to which licensed suppliers must submit eBDN data after each delivery
- **Enforcement:** MPA can investigate complaints, inspect documentation and equipment, and sanction suppliers for non-compliance

## 3. Key Singapore bunkering standards

### 3.1 SS 648 — Code of Practice for Bunkering (Mass Flow Metering)

SS 648 is the central standard for conventional fuel bunkering in Singapore. It covers the use of Mass Flow Meters (MFMs) as the authoritative measurement method for bunker deliveries.

**Parts of SS 648 (2024 edition):**

- SS 648-1: Requirements for the metering system (hardware, calibration, custody transfer accuracy)
- SS 648-2: Code of practice for the bunkering operation itself (procedures, documentation, eBDN generation)

**Key requirements under SS 648:**

- All conventional fuel deliveries from licensed barges must use an MPA type-approved MFM system
- The MFM is the authoritative measurement — ship's figures are recorded but the MFM figure governs the BDN quantity
- eBDN must be generated from the MFM system data at the completion of every delivery
- Both the barge crew and the receiving vessel's Chief Engineer must sign the eBDN before the barge departs
- The signed eBDN must be submitted to MPA's digital platform within the required timeframe
- A MARPOL retained sample must be taken using the drip method from the MFM sampling point throughout the delivery
- Delivery method rule: when the operation standard is SS 648 or SS 600, the delivery method must be Vessel (i.e. ship-to-ship via bunker barge). Terminal-to-ship and truck-to-ship operations follow different tracks.

### 3.2 SS 600 — Code of Practice for Bunkering (Conventional / General)

SS 600 is an earlier and more general standard for bunkering operations in Singapore. It predates the mandatory MFM requirement and covers broader operational practices.

In current practice, SS 648 has superseded SS 600 for most conventional fuel deliveries from licensed barges. However, SS 600 may still be referenced in older contracts, legacy systems, and for certain delivery types not yet covered by SS 648.

### 3.3 SS 524 — Sampling of Marine Fuel Oils

SS 524 specifies the procedures and requirements for sampling marine fuel oils in Singapore, including:

- Where samples must be taken (the MFM sampling point or approved alternative)
- The continuous drip method requirement
- Sample labelling, sealing, and retention (minimum 12 months by the supplier)
- Sample handling for analysis

SS 524 is the Singapore-specific implementation of good international sampling practice and aligns with MARPOL sample requirements.

### 3.4 SS 709 — Fuel Type and Grade Codes

SS 709 establishes the standardised fuel type codes and grade designations used in Singapore bunkering documentation, including the eBDN and MPA's digital reporting system.

SS 709 maps fuel product types to specific grade codes that align with both ISO 8217 designations and the additional fuel types now common in Singapore (VLSFO, ULSFO, biofuel blends, methanol).

Why this matters: when submitting eBDN data to MPA, suppliers must use the correct SS 709 fuel type code and grade. Mismatches between what was delivered and what is reported cause compliance issues.

## 4. Fuel type codes and grade mapping (SS 709)

SS 709 categorises fuels along several dimensions:

- Conventional vs Biofuel
- Distillate vs Residual
- Sulphur content (range: HSFO >0.5%, VLSFO 0.1–0.5%, ULSFO/ULSMGO <0.1%)
- Viscosity (determines the RM or DM grade sub-classification)

### Selected SS 709 grade examples

| SS 709 Fuel Type Code | Type | Sulphur | Viscosity | ISO 8217 Grade |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| VLSMGO | Conventional distillate | 0.1–0.5% | 1.4–6 mm²/s | DMX/DMA/DMZ |
| ULSMGO | Conventional distillate | <0.1% | 1.4–6 mm²/s | DMX/DMA/DMZ |
| VLSFO380 | Conventional residual | 0.1–0.5% | 120–380 mm²/s | RMG380 |
| ULSFO180 | Conventional residual | <0.1% | 20–180 mm²/s | RME180 |
| HSFO380 | Conventional residual | 0.5–3.5% | 120–380 mm²/s | RMG380 |
| BIO-VLSFO380 | Biofuel residual | 0.1–0.5% | 120–380 mm²/s | RF380 |
| BIO-MGO | Biofuel distillate | 0.5–1.5% | 2–6 mm²/s | DFA/DFZ |
| MMA | Methanol Grade A | — | — | Marine Methanol Grade A |

> **Note:** Biofuel grades use the RF (residual fuel biofuel) and DFA/DFZ (distillate fuel biofuel) designations, distinct from the ISO 8217 RM and DM grades used for conventional fuels.

## 5. MPA's digital bunkering framework (B2G)

MPA has built a Business-to-Government (B2G) digital reporting infrastructure that connects licensed bunker suppliers to the regulator in near real time.

### How it works

1. A bunkering operation is completed aboard the licensed barge
2. The MFM system generates the eBDN data
3. The eBDN is signed digitally by the barge crew and the receiving vessel's Chief Engineer
4. The signed eBDN data is transmitted to MPA's platform via the approved digital submission pathway
5. MPA receives the delivery record and can monitor compliance in near real time

### Data required in MPA submission

Key data fields include:

- Vessel name and IMO number
- Supplier licence number
- Barge name and IMO number
- Delivery date, time, and location
- Fuel type code (SS 709)
- Quantity (MT)
- MFM meter serial number and reading
- eBDN reference number
- Signatures of both parties

## 6. Loading and transfer operations in Singapore

In addition to delivery operations (supplying fuel to a receiving vessel), Singapore standards also cover:

### Loading operations

When a bunker barge is loaded from a terminal or floating storage unit (FSU):

- Delivery method: Vessel or Terminal
- No eBDN is required for the loading operation itself
- A loading BDN and Certificate of Quality are typically provided by the terminal

### Transfer operations

When fuel is transferred between two bunker vessels (e.g. for logistical purposes):

- Delivery method: Vessel only
- eBDN is optional (not mandatorily required for transfer operations, unlike delivery)

## 7. Common compliance points

| Area | Requirement | Consequence of non-compliance |
|---|---|---|
| MFM type approval | MFM system must be MPA type-approved and verified | Delivery cannot proceed; licence at risk |
| eBDN generation | Must be generated from MFM data at completion | Non-compliant delivery record; MPA penalty |
| Signing | Chief Engineer of receiving vessel must sign eBDN | Delivery record incomplete; cannot submit to MPA |
| MARPOL sample | Drip sample taken from approved sampling point | PSC deficiency; regulatory exposure |
| B2G submission | Submitted within required timeframe after delivery | Late submission penalty |
| Fuel type code | Correct SS 709 code must be used | Reporting error; possible investigation |
