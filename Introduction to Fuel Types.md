This guide gives a practical overview of the main fuel types used in shipping. It is written for people who are new to maritime.

## 1. Why fuel types matter

Fuel choice affects cost, range, safety, maintenance, and emissions. Different fuels require different engines, tanks, and handling procedures. Regulations — sulphur caps, emission targets, safety codes — strongly influence which fuels can be used where.

In bunkering and maritime operations, you will constantly see trade-offs between:

- Energy density – how much energy per unit volume or weight
- Fuel price and availability
- Environmental impact – CO₂, sulphur, NOₓ, methane slip, etc.
- Technical maturity and infrastructure – can we bunker this fuel in a given port?

## 2. Core fuel characteristics

Learn these four concepts first. They explain why ships still mainly use oil-based fuels today, and what changes with gas and new fuels.

### 2.1 Energy density

How much energy a fuel stores per unit of volume or mass. Higher energy density means smaller tanks or longer range for the same voyage. Conventional fuel oil has high energy density; LNG and ammonia have lower energy density by volume and require larger or specially designed tanks.

### 2.2 Energy efficiency

How much of the fuel's energy is converted into useful work (moving the ship) rather than wasted as heat. Higher efficiency means less fuel burned for the same voyage. Influenced by engine type and tuning, hull design, weather, speed profile, and operating practices.

### 2.3 Flashpoint

The lowest temperature at which a fuel's vapour can ignite. Lower flashpoint means more flammable, requiring stricter handling rules, specific tank design, ventilation, and fire-fighting systems. MGO has a flashpoint >60°C; methanol is considerably lower.

### 2.4 Sulphur content

Amount of sulphur in the fuel, measured in % m/m or ppm. Burning sulphur produces SOₓ emissions, which cause air pollution and acid rain. The IMO 2020 global sulphur cap (0.5% outside ECAs, 0.1% inside ECAs) drives demand for low-sulphur fuels, scrubbers, or alternative fuels.

## 3. Main fuel families in shipping

Most ships still run on oil-based fuels (MFO, MGO). LNG is the primary cleaner transition fuel. Biofuels, methanol, ammonia, hydrogen, electricity, and nuclear power are part of the longer-term decarbonisation mix.

### 3.1 Conventional oil-based fuels

**Heavy Fuel Oil (HFO / HSFO)**

- Residual, heavy fuel — traditionally the workhorse of deep-sea shipping.
- High energy density and historically lower cost than distillates.
- High sulphur unless in very-low-sulphur grades; more impurities and a risk of engine issues if quality is poor. Requires heating and treatment (purifiers).
- ISO 8217 grades: RMA–RMK series (residual).

**Marine Gas Oil (MGO / ULSMGO / VLSMGO)**

- Distillate fuel, similar to diesel.
- Cleaner, more stable, lower sulphur, simpler handling (no heating required).
- More expensive than heavy fuel.
- ISO 8217 grades: DMX, DMA, DMZ, DMB series (distillate).

**VLSFO (Very Low Sulphur Fuel Oil)**

- Max 0.5% sulphur — the most common fuel after IMO 2020.
- Can be residual or a blend, depending on the supplier.
- ISO 8217 grades vary; typically RME/RMG class.

**MDO (Marine Diesel Oil)**

- A blend of distillate and residual fuel. Grades include DMB.
- Used in medium-speed engines.

### 3.2 LNG (Liquefied Natural Gas)

- Natural gas cooled to −162°C to become a liquid, reducing its volume by approximately 600 times.
- Lower CO₂ emissions per unit of energy vs conventional fuel oil (~20% reduction).
- Very low sulphur and particulate matter — well within ECA requirements.
- Requires insulated cryogenic tanks and specialised safety and handling systems.
- Methane slip (unburned methane reaching the atmosphere) is a climate concern and an area of active engine development.
- Well-developed safety standards (IGC Code, IMO IGF Code for gas-fuelled ships).

### 3.3 Biofuels (B24, B30, B100, HVO)

- Blends of bio-based fuel with conventional marine fuel, or 100% renewable.
- Typical labels: B24 (24% bio), B30 (30% bio), B100 (100% biofuel).
- HVO (Hydrotreated Vegetable Oil) is a drop-in biofuel with properties close to MGO.
- Can often be used in existing engines with limited or no modification.
- Potential lifecycle CO₂ reductions if sustainably sourced; feedstock and sustainability questions remain.
- Fuel quality and stability must be carefully managed.

### 3.4 Methanol

- Liquid at ambient conditions — easier to store than LNG.
- Lower local air pollutants vs heavy fuel; liquid handling is simpler than cryogenic.
- Toxic and flammable — strong safety procedures required.
- Lower energy density than conventional fuels; engines require modification.
- Available in conventional or green (bio-methanol / e-methanol) variants.

### 3.5 Ammonia

- Seen as a potential future zero-carbon fuel (no carbon in the molecule).
- Could achieve very low lifecycle CO₂ if produced from green hydrogen and renewable energy.
- Toxic and corrosive — significant safety challenges.
- Very low energy density by volume.
- Engine and supply chain technologies still emerging; not yet commercially deployed at scale.

### 3.6 Electricity (batteries)

- Used today mainly in short-sea, ferries, and harbour craft.
- Zero local emissions from the vessel.
- Very low energy density → significant range limitations.
- Depends on shore charging infrastructure and the carbon intensity of the grid.

### 3.7 Hydrogen

- Can be used in fuel cells or internal combustion engines.
- Potential zero-carbon fuel if produced from renewable energy (green hydrogen).
- Major storage and infrastructure challenges (compressed or cryogenic liquid).
- Safety and bunkering infrastructure still developing.

### 3.8 Nuclear

- Used in naval and icebreaker fleets.
- Extremely high energy density and effectively unlimited range.
- Complex regulation, public acceptance challenges, and specialised crew requirements.

## 4. Fuel grades and ISO 8217

ISO 8217 is the international standard specifying marine fuel quality. It defines two main categories:

**Distillate grades (DM- series):**

- DMX, DMA, DMZ — marine gas oils, typically used in high-speed engines, emergency generators, and ECAs.
- DMB — marine diesel oil, suitable for medium-speed engines.

**Residual grades (RM- series):**

- RMA20 through RMK700 — cover a range of viscosities from 20 cSt to 700 cSt.
- Higher numbers indicate higher viscosity and typically heavier/cheaper grades.
- E.g. RMG380 = residual fuel with maximum viscosity of 380 cSt.

**Key quality parameters specified in ISO 8217:**

- Viscosity (cSt at 50°C for residuals)
- Sulphur content (% m/m)
- Density
- Flashpoint (minimum 60°C for most grades)
- Pour point, water content, ash content, compatibility

> **Note on biofuel grades:** ISO 8217:2024 (the latest edition) includes provisions for blends with bio-components. Singapore's SS709 standard further defines fuel type codes used in digital bunkering submissions.

## 5. Safety, standards, and why they exist

Safety standards exist to protect people, ships, cargo, and the environment. Every fuel type has specific rules for storage, transfer, and use. Standards also underpin commercial trust — buyers and sellers know what they are getting.

When we talk about fuel safety and standards, three things matter:

- **Quantity** — Measuring the right volume or mass of fuel delivered. Important for billing, tax, and emissions reporting.
- **Quality** — Ensuring the fuel meets a defined specification. Poor quality can lead to engine damage, tank issues (sludge, corrosion), and reduced energy efficiency.
- **Safety** — Preventing fires, explosions, spills, and health risks. Designing systems and procedures for safe handling, storage, transfer, and emergency response.

**Key standards and references:**

- ISO 8217 – Marine fuel quality specification
- SS648 / SS600 / SS709 – Singapore standards for bunkering mass flow metering, operations, and fuel grade coding
- IMO IGF Code – International code for ships using gases or other low-flashpoint fuels (LNG, methanol)
- MARPOL Annex VI – Limits sulphur content and NOₓ emissions

## 6. Fuel choice and carbon emissions

Carbon emissions from ships depend on how much fuel is burned and what type of fuel is used.

**Two levers:**

1. Improve energy efficiency – burn less fuel for the same voyage.
2. Switch to lower-carbon or zero-carbon fuels – change the fuel type.

**From an emissions perspective:**

- Higher sulphur → more SOₓ, but sulphur itself is not carbon.
- Fuels with more hydrogen and less carbon per unit of energy generally reduce CO₂ per unit of work — e.g. LNG emits ~20% less CO₂ per unit of energy than HFO, but methane slip must be managed.
- Biofuels can reduce lifecycle CO₂ depending on feedstock and production method.

Over time, the industry is shifting from high-carbon, high-sulphur fuels towards low-carbon and zero-carbon options, driven by new regulations (IMO GHG strategy, EU ETS, FuelEU Maritime) and market pressure.

## 7. Quick reference: key fuel types

| Fuel | Type | Sulphur | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| HSFO | Residual | >0.5% | Requires scrubber outside ECAs |
| VLSFO | Residual/blend | ≤0.5% | Most common post-IMO 2020 |
| MGO / ULSMGO | Distillate | ≤0.1% | Clean, required in ECAs |
| MDO | Distillate/blend | Varies | Medium-speed engines |
| LNG | Gas (cryogenic) | Negligible | Lower CO₂, requires special tanks |
| Biofuel (BXX) | Bio-blend | Varies | Drop-in potential, sustainability questions |
| HVO | Bio-distillate | Low | Drop-in for MGO |
| Methanol | Liquid | None | Toxic, lower energy density |
| Ammonia | Gas/liquid | None | Future fuel, still developing |
